How becoming financially
secure will improve your wellness

    NB this intervention offers general advice and is not a substitute for a qualified financial adviser

  • At its most basic level, money represents security (literally the roof over our heads and food on the table) for our loved ones and us.
  • Money is a valuable resource. So are our time, our relationships, and our lives. Frequently we trade too much time, love and life in return for money.
  • Money worries, current or future can niggle away at us. They are an underlying source of anxiety that erode our present and keep us awake at night.
  • Lack of money limits our choices and keeps us “trapped” in living lives we do not enjoy. Money problems are completely solvable.
  • The real reason people experience financial stress is not because their incomes are too small, but because their spending habits are too big. This is why many people with high incomes have serious financial worries, and why some millionaires and lottery winners end up bankrupt. If you regularly spend more than you earn, you will be in debt, stressed, and frequently unhappy. Eventually, you will be poor or bankrupt.
  • How can you learn to manage your money? There are four simple steps to follow. You may already have completed some of them. Successful completion of all four ensures that not only do you live debt free, consistently spending less than you earn, but that you are on a sustainable and measurable track to achieving total financial freedom.
  • Financial freedom is when you have enough money from passive sources to live on for the rest of your life. You work only because you want to. Don’t wait until retirement.
  • This goal is doable within ten to fifteen years, regardless of your current age or financial position. The steps assume current income. You can considerably accelerate the journey by making good choices about how to spend future increases in income.
  • Financial security is a choice, and is the result of work, not positive thinking. It requires healthy habits, good beliefs, and effective tools.
  • How to become financially secure

    • Taking Control

  • When it comes to money, ignorance is not bliss. Getting organised is one of the keys to financial security.
  • Keep all your financial data in ONE place.
  • All you need is a single ring binder divided into sections, plus plastic wallets for smaller items.
  • Sections could include, Bank Statements: Credit Card Statements: Store Card Statements.
  • Money Received (pay slips or invoices if self employed, benefits, income from investments and/or property, royalty statements, tax rebates etc).
  • Money Out (a list of all direct debits and standing orders, all loan and/or Hire Purchase agreements, utility bills, mortgage statements, receipts or invoices for purchases, cash withdrawal slips etc): Insurance Policies: Tax Information: Savings and Investments: Pension Plans.
  • List assets, liabilities, income, and expenses. Use this information to calculate your net worth. Recalculate every six months.
  • Prepare a cash-flow forecast. A simple method is to use an Excel spreadsheet that tracks your bank account and feeds into a twelve-month cash-flow forecast.
  • SCHEDULE a WEEKLY time slot to update your financial paperwork. Two hours a week maximum (not a lot relative to how much time you put into acquiring money) should be enough to manage your money.
  • Enlist your partner’s help if possible

    • Getting out of Debt

  • Start paying cash for everything: today. If you cannot pay cash, use Switch instead. (Typically, spending drops by around 20% a month.)
  • Find out what you really spend. For seven days, keep a small notebook with you at all times, and write down EVERYTHING you spend.
  • List all store and credit cards, for each one, record the balance outstanding and the current rate of interest.
  • Take the credit card with the lowest interest rate. Keep it with you for emergency use only
  • Take the most expensive credit card and cut it up into small pieces. Notice and celebrate how liberating this feels!
  • Cut up ALL your store cards. Retail therapy does not work.
  • If you are feeling brave cut up all the rest of your credit cards. If you are feeling less brave, seal them in an envelope and hide them under a floorboard or in another inaccessible place.
  • Find out the interest rate for each debt. Focus on paying off the most expensive debt first. Maintain minimum payments on everything else.
  • Remember debt is a serious issue, you do not have to resolve this alone. Debt ruins relationships and lives.

    • Saving Money

  • Stop consuming every £1 you make. Until you deal with expenditure you will never earn enough money to become financially free (not even if you are a millionaire).
  • Starting today, SAVE a proportion of every pound that comes your way. You can begin with as little as 1% and increase by 1% a month for a year. To be firmly on the track to financial freedom, the ultimate goal is to save 20% of income.
  • Identify ten ways in which money is spent that is not really rewarding. Take immediate and radical action to cut expenditure by 10 to 40%
  • Use store and credit cards ONLY for convenience and ONLY IF you absolutely always pay back the balance each month without incurring interest. Remember that Dee Hock who invented Visa card in the late 60s got rid of all his credit cards years ago.
  • If you have a mortgage, make sure it is a capital repayment one and reduce it fast. The word mortgage literally means “death grip” or “death promise”. Your mortgage debt is your liability and your mortgage lender’s asset. Small overpayments every month significantly reduce your mortgage millstone in terms of years and interest payable. Check out mortgage calculators at www.eloan.com. The Mortgage Amortization Calculator displays the breakdown between principal and interest in payments on your loan
  • Whenever you are tempted to take out a loan or buy on HP, ask yourself “What is the total cost?” NOT “Can I afford the monthly repayment?
  • Keep your overheads under control. If they rise with income, you end up stuck in a job you do not want, doing things with your life you would rather not be doing.
  • Pay yourself FIRST. Set up an automatic mandate on your bank account, so that money is transferred automatically to your savings account. Otherwise you pay everyone else (tax, mortgage, car, insurances, loans, utilities, etc) first, leaving nothing left to save.
  • Build up a cash security reserve equivalent to somewhere between 3 to 12 months living expenses. The right amount is the one that makes you feel financially secure.

    • Investing Wisely

  • Now you have ready access to all the financial data about yourself and are financially secure. Now you have amassed a cash reserve, you regularly save more than you earn and your spending is under control. Now you are living well within your means without suffering, THEN you are ready to invest wisely for your future.
  • Spending gives a temporary payback. Wealthy people are wealthy because they put an extra step between earning and spending: investing. Investment gives an ongoing return.
  • Do not invest in anything until you have formulated your personal plan to achieve financial freedom.
  • Remember that there are typically four main ways of creating real wealth: the stock market, property investment, profiting from your passion, and your own business.
  • Establish your personal vision of financial freedom. What does it feel like, look like, smell like, and taste like? How will you live your life? What dreams will you make come true?
  • Hire a personal financial coach to help you create a compelling vision of your financial future, and to overcome any self-limiting beliefs and emotions about money. Financial freedom depends upon healthy beliefs, behaviours and attitudes.
  • Make sure that you educate yourself, consider all options, and seek comprehensive, professional advice. Consult an Independent Financial Advisor, your accountant, your bank manager, your mortgage broker, and your pension fund manager, successful investors, business owners and property professionals. Consider the possibility that the best advice comes from people who themselves are firmly on the track to financial freedom
  • Be wary of other people’s agendas, particularly those with products or services to sell.
  • Learn and apply the miracle of what Albert Einstein called the eighth wonder of the world: COMPOUND INTEREST. Suppose that when he arrived in the New World in 1492, Christopher Columbus had deposited $1 into a savings account that paid 5% a year in SIMPLE interest. Each year he would earn 5 cents on his dollar. By 2002 his one-dollar would have grown to $26.50. Now suppose that he had taken that same dollar and placed it in a savings account that paid 5% COMPOUND interest. That onedollar would now be worth more than $55 billion dollars. (Example from J. J. Pritchard’s “Quest for the Pillars of Wealth”)
